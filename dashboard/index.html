📜 Historical Performance (1928–2026)

98 years of returns data covering 14+ market cycles, depressions, wars, recessions, and technology booms

Long-Run Resilience

Despite 14 bear markets, 3 financial crises, 2 world wars, and multiple recessions, $1 invested in 1928 grew to over $8,200 by 2026 (nominal). Real return: ~$80 inflation-adjusted.

Sequence-of-Returns Risk

A retiree who started drawing in 1929 or 2000 faced very different outcomes than one who started in 1982. Entry timing and withdrawal sequencing matter enormously.

Decade Variation is Huge

The 1970s returned near 0% real; the 1990s returned ~18% nominal CAGR. Planning around the long-run average ignores decade-scale regime shifts.

🔬 Market Fundamentals

Valuation ratios, earnings yield, and fundamental drivers — the bedrock of long-term return forecasting

CAPE Overvaluation Warning

At 34.1×, the Shiller CAPE is 114% above its historical median of 15.9×. Historical research (Shiller 2015) shows CAPE above 30× typically precedes below-average 10-year real returns of 0–3%. This is the single most reliable long-run valuation predictor available.

Dividend Yield Signal

At 1.3%, the S&P 500 dividend yield is near its all-time low. Historically, low dividend yields predict lower total returns. Buybacks compensate partially, but buyback yields depend on continued earnings.

Earnings Quality Caveat

S&P 500 profit margins are at historically elevated levels (~10.3% vs. 6% long-run avg). Mean reversion in margins is a key structural headwind for long-run earnings growth projections.

🌐 Macro Environment

Interest rates, inflation, GDP growth, and unemployment — the four pillars of the market backdrop

Rate Regime Shift

After 13 years of near-zero rates (2009–2022), the US entered a higher-for-longer rate environment. The Fed Funds rate at 4%+ structurally reduces equity valuations by raising the discount rate applied to future earnings.

GDP-Earnings Long-Run Anchor

Over multi-decade periods, corporate earnings cannot sustainably grow faster than nominal GDP. With US nominal GDP growth expected at ~4.5–5% (2.3% real + ~2.5% inflation), this creates a ceiling on long-run earnings growth.

Inflation Normalization

After peaking at 8% in 2022, CPI has moderated toward 2.8–3%. Stable inflation near 2–3% has historically been a favorable macro backdrop for equities — better than deflation or high inflation regimes.

🏗️ Sector Analysis

S&P 500 sector composition, 10-year return leaders, and concentration risk

Concentration Risk

Information Technology now represents 32% of the S&P 500 — the highest single-sector concentration since the 2000 Dot-Com bubble when Tech hit ~35%. The top 7 stocks (Mag 7) account for ~30% of total market cap, creating significant idiosyncratic risk.

Sector Rotation Cycles

Historically, sector leadership rotates with economic cycles: Tech leads in expansion, Utilities/Staples outperform in contractions. After a decade of Tech dominance, mean reversion in sector weights is a plausible long-run scenario.

Energy's Structural Shift

Energy has fallen from ~13% of S&P 500 in 2008 to 3.6% today. The 10-year CAGR (4.1%) is the lowest of any sector. ESG pressures and energy transition create long-run structural headwinds.

🔭 30-Year Forecast (2026–2056)

Four independent quantitative models project the S&P 500 forward. Read as scenario analysis, not prediction.

Critical Advisory on All 30-Year Forecasts

No model reliably predicts 30-year market outcomes. The spread between models below is intentionally large — it is the honest answer. The most important variable (geopolitical shocks, technological disruption, climate policy) cannot be modeled. Use the range, not the point estimate.

Nominal vs Real Returns

All projected S&P 500 levels are nominal. At 3% annual inflation, a 2056 level of 30,000 is worth only ~12,300 in today's dollars. The real return matters more than the headline number for purchasing power.

Why CAPE Matters Long-Run

Starting valuation is the strongest predictor of long-run returns. At CAPE 34×, mean reversion to 16× over 20 years subtracts ~3.7% annually from nominal returns — a massive drag. The GDP-Linked and CAPE models reflect this headwind explicitly.

30-Year Structural Factors to Watch
Key variables that will determine which projection scenario materializes
AI & Productivity Tailwind

If AI-driven productivity gains add 0.5–1% to long-run GDP growth, the historical mean model would prove correct. This is the key upside risk to the conservative models.

Debt & Fiscal Trajectory

US federal debt at 120% of GDP creates a structural headwind. Rising debt service crowds out investment; potential dollar weakness or credit event could compress valuations.

Demographic Shift

Baby Boomer wealth drawdown peaks in the 2030s. Forced selling of equities by retirees may structurally depress P/E multiples — a factor absent from historical models built on accumulation eras.

Geopolitical Reordering

US-China decoupling, weaponization of the dollar, and trade fragmentation all carry long-run earnings implications that no quantitative model adequately captures. Scenario planning is prudent.
